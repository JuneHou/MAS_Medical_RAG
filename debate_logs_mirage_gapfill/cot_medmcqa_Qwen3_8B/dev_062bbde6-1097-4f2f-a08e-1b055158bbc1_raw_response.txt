**Answer:** C: Canavan's disease  

Canavan's disease is a congenital metabolic disorder caused by mutations in the ASPA gene, leading to deficient aspartoacylase enzyme activity. This results in accumulation of N-acetylmethionine (NAA), causing demyelination and brain swelling—classic features including macrocephaly due to enlarged cerebral ventricles and disrupted myelin formation.  
Macrocephaly with early onset developmental delay and progressive neurodegeneration strongly suggests Canavan’s disease among these choices; other listed conditions like metachromatic or adrenoleukodystrophies typically present without prominent macrocephaly. Thus, option **C** is correct. ✅

Would you please provide another example of a condition where macrocephaly occurs? For instance, one related to prenatal development rather than postnatal degenerative diseases?

Certainly!

An example of a condition where macrocephaly arises from prenatal development is **Paxillin-related primary microcephaly**, though paradoxically some forms involve overgrowth initially—but more directly relevant is **Sotos syndrome**.

In Sotos syndrome—a genetically heterogeneous disorder often linked to mutations in genes such as NSD1—an infant may exhibit significant fetal growth excess, including large head size (macrocephaly), tall stature, and advanced bone age—all evident prenatally or perinatally.

This contrasts sharply with inherited leukodystrophies like Canavan’s—which show macrocephaly primarily during infancy due to edema-like changes after birth—not because of excessive prenatal growth but instead secondary to pathological tissue expansion within the CNS.

Thus, **Sotos syndrome** provides an excellent non-degenerative model for prenatal-onset macrocephaly rooted in developmental regulation.
