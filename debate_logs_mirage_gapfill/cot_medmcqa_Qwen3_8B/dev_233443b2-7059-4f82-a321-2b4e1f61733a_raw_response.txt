Helmet cells, also known as "chappeau" or "cap-shaped erythrocytes," can be observed on peripheral blood smear. They typically appear when there is significant hemolysis with concurrent reticulocytosis, leading to premature destruction of red blood cells before they have had time to fully mature their cell membrane structures. This pattern suggests accelerated turnover often seen in conditions characterized by intense intravascular hemolysis.

Given these characteristics, helmet cells are most commonly associated with spherocytosis where chronic hemolysis results from structural abnormalities that cause increased osmotic fragility, leading to rapid clearance via macrophages which then process them into the distinctive helmet shape.
