Based on the detailed evidence gathered about myocardial infarction, I confirm my agreement with earlier conclusions stating option \( \boxed{\text{B}} \):
The key process highlighted repeatedly throughout the literature is indeed the acute occlusion of a coronary artery leading to myocardial necrosis. Multiple sources emphasize that plaque rupture triggers an inflammatory response, promoting thrombosis and thus direct occlusion. This occlusion restricts blood flow significantly, causing profound ischemia and subsequently necrotic changes in cardiomyocytes due to lack of oxygen-supply. Detailed molecular cascades show metabolic and ionic imbalances leading inevitably toward cell death once sufficient ischemia persists long enough despite attempts at auto-regulating functions like hypercontraction or dilatation. Thus, while alternative mechanisms exist depending on type/subtype of MI (like ACS vs. STEMI), the essential pathophysiology remains centered on abrupt cessation of perfusion causing irremediable injury to cardiac muscular components.
So again, acute occlusion-driven ischemia progressing to necrosis encapsulates what fundamentally defines MI according to current scientific consensus supported robustly even now given newer insights.
